1 Introduction

Bitcoin is highly volatile. This project analyzes its price movements over the last year and tests whether the Black-Scholes model can price options on it.

The main things we had to do were:

  • Download and clean one year of Bitcoin data.
  • Calculate risk numbers like volatility and kurtosis.
  • Price European Call and Put options.
  • Do everything twice: once in Excel and once in VBA to check our work.

2 Finding and Preparing the Data

2.1 Source

We got the daily data for BTC-USD from Yahoo Finance. We used a period of one year.

2.2 Cleaning the data

The data wasn't perfect when we downloaded it. We had to fix a few things:

  • The dates were stored as text.
  • We sorted the data from oldest to newest so the returns would be correct.
  • We checked for missing values.

2.3 Log returns

We used logarithmic returns because they are better for adding up over time:

$$r_{t}=ln(\frac{P_{t}}{P_{t-1}})$$

3 Statistics

3.1 Annualized Volatility

Because Bitcoin trades daily, including weekends, we annualized volatility using 365 days instead of the standard 252.

$$\sigma_{annual}=\sigma_{daily}\times\sqrt{365}$$

3.2 Skewness and Kurtosis

We also looked at the shape of the distribution. Skewness tells us if the returns lean to one side. Kurtosis tells us about the tails. We found that Bitcoin has high positive excess kurtosis. This means "fat tails", so extreme price jumps happen more often than a normal model predicts.

4 Black-Scholes Option Pricing

4.1 Formulas

We used the standard formulas for European options:

$$C=S_{0}N(d_{1})-Ke^{-rT}N(d_{2})$$

$$P=Ke^{-rT}N(-d_{2})-S_{0}N(-d_{1})$$

4.2 Limits

Black-Scholes assumes volatility never changes, which is not true for Bitcoin. So these prices are just a theoretical baseline.
